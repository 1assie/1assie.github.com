временно здесь| this will go elsewhere

When we are talking about faults, we use may/might/could to list the possible reasons; should/ought to talk about what we expect to happen; can't to exclude various reasons; and must when we decide what reason for the fault is.

I can't get a dialling tone on my phone. It may be because there is a fault in the phone, or it could be in British Telecom's equipment, or I might not [order!] have plugged the phone in. So I check the plug. It's in the socket. So it can't be that. British Telecom say their equipment is OK. The phone ought to work, but it doesn't. The fault must be somewhere in the phone itself.

My car doesn't start one morning. Why not? There are a number of possible explanations. It may/could/might be the battery. It may/could/might be the plugs. So I check them. Both of them are OK, so the car should/ought to start. But it doesn't. I put a new starter motor lat week so it can't be that. Then I notice that the needle in the petrol gauge is pointing at zero. It must be the petrol. The tank must be empty. If I put some petrol in, it should/ought to start. The petrol gauge may/could/might be faulty, but I don't think it is.

NB "Degrees of certainty"
